Mobility App Experience Usability Research

Usability Research for a Vehicle-Sharing Platform

Evaluated and improved the end-to-end user experience of a leading vehicle-sharing service — diagnosing friction across core task flows and delivering evidence-based recommendations to improve usability, conversion, and user confidence.

Role UX Researcher
Industry Mobility & Transportation
Methods Usability Testing, Task Analysis
Focus End-to-End Flow Evaluation

Context

A major vehicle-sharing platform — one of the most widely used short-term car rental services in its market — sought an independent evaluation of its mobile app experience. Despite strong market penetration, the product team had observed unexpectedly high task abandonment rates at specific points in the booking and vehicle-return flows, and wanted to understand the underlying usability causes before committing to redesign work.

Vehicle-sharing presents a distinctive UX challenge: the app must guide users through a real-world physical process — locating a vehicle, unlocking it, completing paperwork, and returning it — often under conditions of time pressure, unfamiliar environments, and divided attention. Interface failures here have direct operational consequences.

Research Design

Task-Based Usability Testing

I designed and facilitated a series of moderated usability tests covering the end-to-end service journey: account registration and verification, vehicle discovery and reservation, pickup flow (including vehicle inspection and unlock), in-trip interactions, and the return and billing closure process. Participants were recruited across experience levels — first-time users, occasional renters, and frequent users — to surface both onboarding failures and habitual friction that experienced users had normalized.

Testing was conducted in realistic conditions where possible: participants interacted with the app in contexts simulating actual use (time constraints, unfamiliar locations, varied device sizes) rather than in sterile lab settings. This ecological validity was essential for surfacing friction that only emerges when cognitive load is high.

Research Methods

  • Moderated usability testing across full booking-to-return task flow
  • Think-aloud protocol with concurrent probe questioning
  • Task completion rate and error logging by flow segment
  • Post-task confidence and satisfaction measurement (SEQ, SUS)
  • Heuristic evaluation as a supplementary analytical lens

Critical Findings

Testing identified a cluster of high-severity usability issues concentrated in two areas: the identity verification onboarding (a mandatory, multi-step process involving document upload and regulatory compliance steps) and the vehicle return flow (where users had to complete a sequence of physical and in-app actions within a constrained time window). In both cases, the app provided insufficient feedback during processing states, leading users to make repeated, erroneous inputs — compounding both their frustration and actual error rates.

A secondary pattern emerged around navigation — experienced users had developed personal workarounds for features they found non-intuitive, indicating accumulated usability debt that first-time users had no mechanism to overcome.

Recommendations & Impact

Findings were structured into a severity-tiered recommendation report, prioritizing interventions by their impact on conversion and task completion. High-priority recommendations addressed processing feedback states, error recovery language, and the step sequencing of the verification onboarding. Lower-priority items covered navigation consistency and edge-case error handling.

Key Recommendations

  • Redesign processing feedback across identity verification to prevent repeated submission errors
  • Restructure vehicle return flow sequencing to match users' physical-digital action order
  • Clarify error messaging with actionable recovery language across all critical task steps
  • Simplify new-user onboarding by separating regulatory requirements from product orientation
  • Address navigation inconsistencies that disadvantage first-time users relative to habituated ones

Reflection

This project was a clear reminder that mobility apps are not just software experiences — they are interfaces for physical, time-sensitive actions in the real world. Designing and testing them in clinical conditions alone produces findings that don't travel. The most useful research decisions I made here were about ecological validity: introducing realistic constraints into the test context so that what we observed actually resembled what users experienced on the street.

It also reinforced a professional principle I return to often: the highest-severity usability problems are rarely the most visually obvious ones. They hide in the processing states, the error messages, and the moments between screens — the parts of an app that only reveal themselves when something goes wrong.
