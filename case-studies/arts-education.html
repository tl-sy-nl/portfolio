Arts & Culture Digital Transformation User Research

Digital Classroom Experience for a Renowned Performing Arts Organization

Researched and designed the digital learning experience for a nationally celebrated performing arts institution — helping a culturally revered organization translate its deeply physical, embodied pedagogy into an accessible, meaningful online classroom.

Role Senior UX Researcher
Industry Performing Arts & Cultural Education
Methods User Interviews, Usability Testing, Service Design
Scope Digital Transformation of Educational Programs

The Challenge

A nationally celebrated performing arts organization — renowned for decades of contribution to contemporary dance and cultural education — faced a fundamental tension when expanding its educational programs online. Its pedagogy is embodied and relational by nature: learning happens through physical presence, rhythmic imitation, and the intimate dynamic between instructor and student. The question was not simply how to digitize content, but how to preserve the essence of that learning relationship in a medium that lacks physical co-presence.

The organization's learners ranged from young children experiencing dance for the first time to adult practitioners with years of embodied practice. Each segment brought distinct motivations, physical contexts, and expectations of what "learning" should feel like online.

Research Approach

Understanding the Embodied Learning Context

I began with in-depth interviews across learner segments — children, parents coordinating family participation, adult practitioners, and instructors navigating the shift to online teaching. A critical insight emerged early: the perceived value of this organization's education was not solely in the dance technique itself, but in the sense of belonging to a cultural community and the distinctive pedagogical identity of its instructors. Any digital product that reduced this to video playback alone would fail to retain students.

I also conducted observational research of existing online class sessions to identify specific moments of friction — where the digital medium actively disrupted the teaching dynamic — and moments of resilience, where instructors had found creative workarounds worth formalizing into the platform design.

Research Methods

  • In-depth interviews with learners across age groups, parents, and instructors
  • Observational research of live and recorded online sessions
  • Contextual inquiry into at-home learning environments and physical constraints
  • Usability testing of the digital classroom interface across device types
  • Service blueprint mapping of the full learner journey (discovery → enrollment → ongoing engagement)

Reframing the Design Problem

Research revealed that the primary barrier to online learning engagement was not technical friction in the platform itself, but motivational continuity — learners who were deeply committed in-person struggled to sustain the same ritual and intention in a home environment. This reframed the design challenge: rather than optimizing for feature completeness, we needed to design for ritual, community, and the preservation of pedagogical identity.

I translated this into a set of design principles that guided the team: the platform should surface the instructor's presence and personality, not obscure it behind an interface; it should give learners structure and progress markers that mirror the psychological progression of in-person study; and it should acknowledge the physical environment as a real constraint to be designed around, not ignored.

Experience Strategy & Design Recommendations

Instructor-Led Presence Over Passive Content

The research strongly indicated that learners associated quality and legitimacy with the live, relational aspect of the organization's classes. I recommended a platform architecture that foregrounded synchronous and instructor-led asynchronous formats over purely on-demand video — structuring the product around cohort-based learning journeys with clear temporal rhythms rather than an open library of independent content.

Designing for the At-Home Physical Environment

Many learners — particularly children — were practicing in living rooms, bedrooms, and small apartments. The physical space itself was a usability variable that no interface audit would surface. I developed environment-aware design guidelines: prompts for space preparation, device positioning guidance embedded in the onboarding flow, and course content structured with explicit acknowledgment of spatial constraints.

Onboarding & Learner Confidence

Usability testing highlighted a recurring confidence gap for first-time users: the combination of unfamiliar technology and the vulnerability of learning a physical art form in a newly visible (digital) context created anxiety that caused early dropout. I designed an onboarding sequence that addressed this dual barrier — lowering technical friction while also normalizing imperfection and reinforcing the safety and community of the learning environment from the very first session.

Outcomes

Research and design recommendations were formalized into a service planning document and product brief that the organization's internal team used to guide platform development. The project also produced a reusable learner research framework that the organization could apply in future product iterations.

Key Deliverables & Impact

  • Full-spectrum learner research across age groups and engagement levels
  • Design principles grounded in the organization's pedagogical identity
  • Onboarding experience redesign addressing technical and motivational barriers
  • Service blueprint mapping the learner journey from discovery to retention
  • Environment-aware content and interface guidelines for at-home practice

Reflection

This project deepened my appreciation for what gets lost when we treat "digitization" as a straightforward technical translation. The most important design decisions here were not about the interface — they were about understanding what made this organization's education meaningful in the first place, and what would be irrecoverably lost if we moved too fast. Good research in this case wasn't just a phase in a process; it was the act of protecting something culturally valuable from being flattened by a product roadmap.
